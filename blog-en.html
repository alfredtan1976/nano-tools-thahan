Thai conscription 2026: service reductions by education and Ror Dor

Last updated: June 19, 2026

Thai military service length is not the same for everyone. It depends on education documents, Ror Dor training, and whether the person volunteers or is selected by red card.

  • High school or lower: red card usually means 2 years; volunteering may reduce it to 1 year.
  • Diploma or bachelor's degree: red card may reduce to 1 year if documents are submitted on draft day; volunteering may reduce to 6 months.
  • Ror Dor year 2: red card may reduce to 1 year; volunteering may reduce to 6 months.
  • Ror Dor year 3 or above: generally exempt from draft selection, but documents should still be verified.

The most important rule is document timing. Education documents must be submitted on draft selection day. Late submission may not be accepted and can result in a longer service period.

When do Batch 1 and Batch 2 Thai conscripts discharge?

Last updated: June 17, 2026

The Army and Air Force normally use Batch 1 on May 1 and Batch 2 on November 1. The Navy may use four batches: May 1, August 1, November 1, and February 1.

A discharge date is estimated by adding the required service length to the entry date. Six-month, one-year, and two-year terms depend on education, Ror Dor status, and entry type.

Leave days are temporary rest days and do not automatically make an official discharge date earlier. Disciplinary penalties or absence may affect actual service counting.

What are Sor Dor 9, Sor Dor 35, and Sor Dor 43?

Last updated: June 15, 2026

Sor Dor 9 is the Thai military registration record for eligible males. Sor Dor 35 is the draft summons that states when and where to report for selection. Sor Dor 43 is the selection result certificate.

Sor Dor 43 is especially important for employment and official proof that the person has completed or passed the required military process. It should be kept safely.

Be careful with anyone claiming to provide Sor Dor 43 without attending the official process. False official documents can create serious legal risk.

Thai conscript salary and benefits in 2026

Last updated: June 12, 2026

Thai conscript monthly income is commonly discussed as a combination of base pay, temporary cost-of-living support, and daily allowance. Actual net transfers may vary by unit because of meal costs, uniforms, savings, or other deductions.

Other benefits may include military medical care, education opportunities such as non-formal education, and in some cases a route toward military career exams.

Rights

Student postponement and medical exemptions

Last updated: June 10, 2026

Students may be eligible to postpone draft selection while studying, usually through their school or university. The process should be handled months before draft season, not at the last minute.

Medical exemptions require proper documentation. For many conditions, a military hospital or approved medical process may be needed before selection day.

Even with postponement or medical documents, the person generally still needs to report according to the summons unless officially instructed otherwise.

Thai military draft guide for LGBTQ+ and trans women

Last updated: June 8, 2026

Thai draft selection for LGBTQ+ people and trans women is sensitive and document-heavy. Preparation can reduce stress and help protect privacy and dignity on selection day.

Medical certificates from military hospitals or recognized institutions should be prepared in advance. Supporting documents related to gender-affirming care may also help if applicable.

Reporting on the official date is still important. After the process, the Sor Dor 43 document should be checked and kept safely for future employment or official use.

New Conscript

What happens after drawing a red card? From draft day to recruit training

Published: June 19, 2026

Drawing a red card can create immediate uncertainty. The conscript and family must quickly understand reporting dates, assigned branch, documents, employment arrangements, finances, health preparation, and communication during initial training.

Check every document before leaving selection

Confirm the spelling of the name, identification number, branch, batch, reporting date, and reporting location. Errors should be raised immediately. Photograph the documents and leave a copy with a trusted family member.

Arrange work, education, debt, and housing

Employees should notify employers and request the relevant employment documents. Students should ask their institution about leave of absence and future enrollment. Rent, loan payments, phone bills, and family support should be listed and, where possible, placed on reliable automatic payment.

A family emergency fund is useful because the first payment cycle may not match expectations. Avoid borrowing from informal lenders to buy excessive training supplies; units often provide standard items or issue their own restrictions.

Prepare health without overtraining

Shift toward an earlier sleep schedule, walk or jog at a moderate level, and address existing injuries before reporting. People who use regular medication should carry clearly labeled medicine, medical records, and certificates for unit medical staff.

Vision, dental, allergy, respiratory, and mental health concerns should be documented honestly. Reporting a real health issue early is safer than hiding it until training makes it worse.

Pack documents first, valuables last

Keep identification, appointment papers, household registration copies, education records, medical records, and emergency contacts in a waterproof folder. Personal items should be simple and labeled.

Do not carry large amounts of cash, expensive jewelry, or unlabeled medication. Check official unit instructions before buying items recommended by social media because rules differ between branches and training centers.

Understand the first days of intake

Initial intake may include document verification, medical checks, assignment, uniform distribution, and unit rules. Phone access can be limited, so families should agree beforehand that several quiet days do not automatically mean an emergency.

During recruit training, severe pain, breathing problems, fainting, or intense psychological distress should be reported to instructors or medical staff immediately. Asking for help when symptoms are real is a safety measure, not a failure.

Create one clear family communication system

Choose one primary family contact and record the unit's official telephone number and mailing information. This reduces repeated calls and conflicting messages. Important information should be confirmed through official channels before being shared online.

Only start the countdown with confirmed details

Use the calculator after the entry date and service length are confirmed. If the unit announces a different official discharge date, follow the unit and update the saved countdown. The tool is intended to support planning and morale, not replace official documents.

Family

A practical guide for military partners and families during conscript service

Published: June 18, 2026

Military service changes the routine of both the person inside the unit and the people waiting at home. Uncertain phone access, changing income, leave dates, and safety concerns can strain relationships. A simple shared system reduces guesswork and emotional pressure.

Set realistic communication expectations

During recruit training, phone access may be limited to certain times. A delayed reply should not automatically be interpreted as rejection or danger. Messages should be short, supportive, and easy to answer when time becomes available.

One family member should serve as the main contact and relay updates to others. This prevents repeated calls to the unit and limits rumors when information is incomplete.

Separate emergencies from problems that can wait

Serious illness, legal deadlines, or housing-threatening financial issues may require urgent contact. Everyday purchases, minor appointments, and temporary worries can be recorded for a calmer conversation later.

The person in training should not be expected to solve every household problem during a short phone call. Families benefit from a separate support network of relatives, trusted friends, or community services.

Build a shared financial plan

List fixed expenses, reducible expenses, the salary account, the person responsible for checking transfers, and a minimum emergency reserve. Net conscript income and payment timing can vary between units, so plans should not rely on one exact payday.

Keep transfer records and ask respectfully for clarification if deductions are unclear. Avoid high-interest borrowing based on an expected salary date.

Treat leave dates as provisional until confirmed

Leave can change because of training, duties, or unit orders. Use refundable travel bookings and wait for confirmation before planning major family events. A leave countdown is helpful for morale, but official unit information takes priority.

Protect emotional wellbeing on both sides

Training can make a conscript tired, quiet, or emotionally flat. Partners at home may feel lonely or insecure. Do not build a relationship conclusion from the tone of one short call. A useful question is: “Do you want me to listen, or help find a solution?”

Persistent insomnia, hopelessness, panic, or thoughts of self-harm require prompt support from medical professionals, mental health services, unit leadership, or trusted family. Do not wait for the next leave period.

Use social media carefully

Avoid posting duty schedules, operational locations, identity documents, bank details, or identifiable information about other conscripts without permission. Countdown cards should share encouragement and remaining time, not sensitive unit information.

Make the countdown meaningful

The falling number can create hope, but life should not become only a wait for the final day. Mark smaller milestones such as completing initial training, payday, approved leave, a study goal, or a post-service job plan. This gives the service period structure and purpose.

Career

Planning work before service and returning to employment after discharge

Published: June 20, 2026

Military service can interrupt income, education, and career momentum for six months, one year, or two years. Most long-term career risk can be reduced by preparing documents, recording skills, maintaining professional relationships, and planning the return to work before reporting for service.

Do not leave employment without written records

Employees should notify employers in writing and attach the summons or reporting document. Request an employment certificate showing the role, dates, salary, and relevant benefits. Save copies of emails and responses somewhere a trusted family member can access.

Do not sign resignation, waiver, or settlement documents that are not fully understood. Questions about employment rights should be raised with human resources, labor authorities, or qualified advisers before signing.

Create a compact career file before access becomes limited

Prepare a one-page resume, work samples, education certificates, training records, and professional references. Save them as PDFs in a secure account rather than only on a phone that may be unavailable during training.

Record measurable details of previous work: number of customers supported, software used, equipment handled, budget responsibility, or processes improved. These details are easily forgotten after a long break.

Translate military experience into civilian skills

Unit duties may develop scheduling, equipment control, driving, documentation, teamwork, first aid, communication, and problem-solving skills. Describe genuine responsibilities in language employers understand without revealing sensitive unit, location, or operational information.

For example, instead of writing only “served in a company,” a truthful description might mention coordinating schedules, maintaining equipment records, or completing time-sensitive administrative work.

Maintain learning continuity

Where unit rules and free time allow, choose one focused subject connected to a career goal: English, basic computing, personal finance, driving, technical work, or public-service examination preparation. Completing one documented course is more useful than starting many courses with no evidence of completion.

Keep the course title, date, learning hours, and certificate in the career file. If internet access is inconsistent, use downloaded material, books, or written study notes.

Build a financial bridge after discharge

Discharge does not guarantee immediate employment. Try to reserve one to three months of essential expenses for travel, rent, food, documents, and job applications. Avoid spending the last savings on a major celebration before a new start date and salary are confirmed.

Review bank accounts, debt, social security, taxes, and any unit savings or unpaid amounts before leaving. Keep records and ask for the correct process to receive outstanding money.

Reconnect with the job market 60-90 days before discharge

Once the official discharge date is confirmed, contact former employers, colleagues, teachers, and professional connections. Explain when work can begin, update the resume, and explore positions without accepting the first offer under pressure.

If interviewing before discharge, be honest about availability and do not promise a start date that the unit has not approved. Clear planning is often viewed positively by employers.

A 30/60/90-day post-service plan

  • First 30 days: recover physically, organize documents, update the resume, review social security, and set a monthly budget.
  • By 60 days: apply for targeted roles, practice interviews, contact professional networks, and close essential skill gaps.
  • By 90 days: review results and adjust job type, location, salary range, or skill evidence instead of repeating the same applications.

Explain military service professionally

There is usually no need to hide the service period and create an unexplained resume gap. State the dates, an appropriate non-sensitive unit description, and relevant responsibilities. Avoid exaggerating rank, missions, or expertise.

In interviews, connect real experience to the role: time discipline, working under pressure, responsibility, and cooperation with people from different backgrounds. A short factual example is stronger than a broad claim such as “I became patient.”

Use discharge as a starting point, not the only finish line

A countdown shows when service ends; a career plan shows what happens next. Track additional milestones such as resume completion, first application date, a certification exam, and an emergency savings target. By discharge day, the conscript can leave with documents, skills, contacts, and a practical next step instead of starting from zero.

Digital Safety

Scam prevention for Thai conscripts and families: impersonation, fake links, and urgent transfers

Published: June 21, 2026

During recruit training, phone access can be irregular and families may know little about the daily schedule. Scammers can exploit this information gap with claims that a conscript is injured, detained, missing equipment, or urgently needs money. A verification system is more reliable than trying to memorize every possible scam.

Create family verification rules before reporting

Agree that urgent money requests must pass through one primary family contact and that nobody transfers money based on one message alone. Choose a private verification question that cannot be guessed from birthdays, identification numbers, or public social media.

Save the unit name, official number, address, and confirmed contacts on at least two family phones. Do not rely only on a number supplied by the person requesting money.

Recognize pressure tactics

  • A demand to transfer within minutes and not contact anyone else
  • Payment for treatment, bail, transport, or equipment to a personal account
  • A new phone number, new social account, or unrelated bank account name
  • Requests for OTP codes, passwords, full identity documents, or banking screenshots
  • Threats of punishment, delayed discharge, or lost rights if payment is not immediate

One warning sign does not prove fraud, but several signs together should trigger a complete stop and independent verification.

Use the stop, separate, and call-back method

Stop replying and do not open the link. Move to a separate channel by calling the conscript, the designated family contact, or an official number saved earlier. Do not use only the callback number provided in the suspicious message.

If confirmation is unavailable, wait unless the emergency can be independently verified through an official organization. A rushed transfer does not make an emergency safer, but a short verification delay can prevent major loss.

Inspect links before entering information

Read the actual domain carefully. Watch for misspellings, extra words, look-alike letters, and subdomains designed to place an official-looking name at the beginning. HTTPS means the connection is encrypted; it does not prove the site belongs to the military or government.

Do not enter personal information through an unsolicited message link. Type a known address manually or use a trusted bookmark. Never install an APK, browser extension, remote-control application, or screen-sharing tool because a stranger says it is required.

Protect military documents and personal data

Sor Dor documents, identity cards, reporting papers, and bank information can be used to create convincing impersonation stories. Do not post full document images in public groups. When a copy is genuinely required, share only through the necessary channel, hide unrelated information, and mark the copy with its purpose and date where appropriate.

Photos from a unit can expose names, identification numbers, schedules, locations, screens, or documents in the background. Review every image before posting.

Secure accounts when phones are stored or shared

Use a strong screen lock and two-factor authentication for email, banking, and social media. Store recovery codes somewhere secure outside the phone and hide message or OTP previews on the lock screen.

Do not share passwords with partners or family “for emergencies.” Instead, document recovery methods and important account contacts without recording the actual passwords.

Watch for cloned social profiles

A scammer may copy a conscript's photos, nickname, and public details to create a new account and request loans from friends. Limit public friend lists and agree that any account or phone-number change must be confirmed through the previous channel.

What to do after clicking or transferring

  1. Contact the bank immediately through its official number and report the suspicious transaction.
  2. Change passwords from a trusted device, starting with the primary email and financial accounts.
  3. Sign out other sessions and remove unknown applications or account permissions.
  4. Preserve chat screenshots, phone numbers, account names, times, amounts, and links.
  5. Report the incident promptly through the relevant official channels.

Do not delete evidence out of embarrassment, and do not send more money to someone promising to recover the first transfer. Fast reporting improves the chance of limiting damage.

A 30-second transfer checklist

  • Did I verify the person through an existing channel?
  • Does the bank-account name match the claimed recipient?
  • Why must this happen immediately, and why am I told not to verify?
  • Am I being asked for an OTP, password, installation, or screen sharing?
  • Would a ten-minute verification call truly create harm?

Share countdowns without sharing sensitive information

A countdown image should focus on progress and an estimated date. Avoid full names, identity numbers, sub-unit details, duty schedules, or current locations. Encouragement can be shared without sacrificing the privacy of the conscript or other people in the unit.

The home support folder before enlistment: documents, money, medical records, and family handover

Published: June 22, 2026

Many people prepare for enlistment by buying supplies, exercising, or talking about leave days. What is often missed is a home support folder: one central set of information that lets the family continue essential tasks when a new conscript has limited phone access. Without it, small issues such as a late bill payment, missing proof for an employer, or confusion over medication can turn into avoidable stress on both sides.

A home support folder is not about giving other people total control. It is about arranging information so the household can function when conversations become shorter and less predictable. When the key facts are kept in one place, family members can act more calmly, verify details, and avoid making rushed mistakes.

1. Build one main folder and one usable backup

Create one waterproof physical folder and one digital copy stored somewhere the family can reliably access. The structure should be simple enough that a relative with no experience handling official papers can find the right section within minutes. Common sections are military documents, identity papers, finances, work or study, and medical information.

The cover page should list the full name, the main family contact, the people allowed to use the folder, and a short inventory of what it contains. Avoid spreading originals and copies across many bags and drawers with no system, because the family may not know which version is current when something urgent happens.

2. Put military and identity documents first

The first section should hold copies of conscription and reporting papers such as Sor Dor 9, Sor Dor 35, Sor Dor 43 if already issued, the Thai ID card, household registration, education records, name-change papers, and appointment or intake documents. Mark each copy with the date it was filed so everyone knows which version belongs to which stage.

If proof is needed for an employer, school, landlord, hospital, or other institution, the family can immediately pull the correct set. Phone photos alone are not enough. Images may be hard to find, deleted by accident, or too poor in quality for printing and formal use.

3. Make a money map that another person can actually follow

Write down every recurring obligation: due date, payment method, account used, and who checks the balance. This can include rent, utilities, phone service, loan payments, family support, and the emergency reserve. If any item is paid automatically, record the bank, app, or provider involved.

The important point is to stop relying on memory and informal habits. During early service, phone calls may be short and infrequent. The family should be able to see which money is reserved, which money is used for monthly expenses, and which bank, service provider, or creditor must be contacted first if there is a problem.

4. Transfer medical information without ambiguity

If the conscript uses regular medication, has a chronic condition, allergies, treatment history, or test results that may matter later, place the medication list, dose, timing, regular hospital, and clinic or doctor contact in the medical section. Do not leave vague notes such as “medicine after meals” without naming the medicine, because that becomes useless in an emergency.

Add copies of important medical certificates, test results, and restrictions such as allergies or prohibited drugs. If medicine must be bought again, record how to obtain it and who can take over the task. The goal is for the household to explain the basics correctly even if the conscript cannot return a call immediately.

5. Work, study, and contracts need a visible handover

Before reporting, gather employment certificates, rental agreements, tuition or enrollment documents, payment schedules, and a list of institutions that may need notice. If something must be followed up later, such as a deposit refund, leave of absence, paperwork from an employer, or an address update, create a separate pending-items list with target dates.

Many problems are not dramatic. They happen because nobody knows who must be called in which week. Writing down the person, telephone number, and purpose of contact in advance reduces missed deadlines and repeated back-and-forth during a period when reaching the conscript may be difficult.

6. Create an emergency contact ladder that reduces panic

The folder should clearly say who receives a money, health, or document problem first, who makes the decision, and which unit or official number has already been verified. This prevents situations where several relatives call at once, share incomplete updates, and create more confusion than the original issue.

It helps to write short family rules directly into the folder. For example, large transfers require one extra verification step from the primary contact, important documents are never sent into public group chats, and emergency news should be confirmed through two established channels before it is forwarded to others.

7. Record account recovery methods, not the actual passwords

If email, banking, or other essential services affect daily life, write down the account name, recovery route, and where identity documents needed for recovery are stored. Do not hand real passwords to multiple people on paper. That solves one short-term problem while creating several new security risks.

The family mainly needs to know where to start if a phone is lost, an account must be locked, or recovery becomes necessary: the primary email, the backup number, or the correct bank branch. This approach protects privacy while still making continuity possible.

8. Review the folder again after intake and before discharge

A home support folder should not be created once and forgotten. During the first two to four weeks after intake, families usually learn which information is used often, which sections are unnecessary, and what should be updated, such as an estimated leave date, payment channel, or extra unit paperwork.

Closer to discharge, add a return-home section for job applications, work history, discharge proof, interview appointments, and accounts that must be checked after leaving the unit. Keeping the folder alive across the full service period makes the transition into and out of military life much smoother.

24-hour checklist before reporting

  • The physical and digital folders both contain military papers, identity documents, household registration, and work or study records.
  • Recurring expenses, due dates, and the responsible person for each payment are clearly listed.
  • Medication, chronic conditions, hospital details, and emergency numbers are written in a format anyone can read quickly.
  • The family knows the primary contact and which channels must be used to verify urgent news.
  • Recovery methods for important accounts are documented without exposing the real passwords.

Some families assume a folder like this is excessive until the day they must find proof quickly, move money under time pressure, or explain treatment history on someone else's behalf. The best preparation is not guessing every future problem correctly. It is making sure the important things do not depend on memory, luck, or panic.
